Week 1 Lecture: VR Dizziness and CAVE

Introduction

Virtual reality can immerse users to the point where they perceive their virtual environment as real, potentially leading to accidents when they forget their actual surroundings. Various solutions have been proposed to mitigate these risks, including auditory warnings, proximity detectors, and removing obstacles in the real world beforehand. However, care must be taken to distinguish these immersion-breaking measures from virtual reality's goal of providing a fully immersive experience.

The CAVE system, unlike head-mounted displays, projects visuals onto the walls of a room, allowing multiple people to view the environment simultaneously. Although it offers a higher level of immersion compared to head-mounted displays, the CAVE System is significantly more expensive and requires a dedicated space for projection.

Safeguards Against Ourselves

There is no shortage of videos and stories of VR users forgetting that what they are seeing and experiencing is not, in fact, real. This is because VR attempts to fool the senses into believing its simulated reality, and because most of the brain is devoted to sight, tricking those parts of the brain is usually enough to make us buy into the simulated reality.

This can cause problems when there are real-world obstacles, such as tables, walls, or wires, that are not replicated in simulated reality. Since the user believes that there is no obstacle, they will run into these obstacles and potentially break them. For example, if a user is playing a VR boxing game, they will simulate punches and kicks by physically doing so. However, they may accidentally punch or kick real-world objects, such as a TV or a table. Not only would this injure the user, but it may also break the objects. To fix this, there are different types of solutions that can be implemented. On the development side, we can program into the VR game a warning system to alert the user that they may crash into some real-world object. This may involve installing proximity detectors to measure the distance between hands and legs to nearby objects. The user can also avoid these situations by removing nearby obstacles, ensuring that they can freely move around without harm.

There's also the issue of dizziness. For the same reasons people get carsick-- namely, the incongruence of seeing motion but not feeling motion-- people get VR dizziness. There is also the fact that while VR attempts to simulate reality, it is not perfect, which can cause the brain to get confused. Examples include a VR headset's FoV being variable and different from a human FoV or a VR headset experiencing latency, causing a mismatch in what should be happening (a shift in perspective) and what is happening (nothing, because the VR headset hasn't updated). This causes the user to be disoriented and possibly lose balance and fall, leading to injury. To fix this, we can remove some visual effects, like motion blur, or discretize movement; instead of smoothly walking from one place to another, a user can teleport from one location to another. Thus, the user never moves continuously, going from being stationary in one place to being stationary at another place. The user can also take breaks, get comfortable, and stay hydrated to reduce dizziness.

These solutions fall into two categories: either make the simulated reality less realistic or let the user self-regulate. The first category is antithetical to the goal of simulated reality, and the second should never be relied upon, yet these stopgap solutions are compromises that both the developer and the user accept in order to be able to have fun with VR.

The CAVE System and HMDs

The CAVE system is a room in which a simulated reality is projected, differing from HMDs in that the simulated reality is not projected onto a helmet or goggles. The user will usually be wearing glasses in the CAVE to enhance the viewing experience. Some advantages are that it allows for a large-scale immersion, allowing for a lower latency in updating the environment when the user wants to look around. It is also less intrusive, in that the user can freely move around in the room, instead of being stuck to an HMD. However, the CAVE requires there to be a room for it to be effective, and there is a large upfront cost in implementing one such system; it is more than an order of magnitude above buying a normal headset.

Within the Reality-Virtuality Continuum of Milgram, it is very much in the Monolithic Computing and Virtual Environment quadrant; more than one person can be in the room to experience the simulation, and the environment is entirely projected onto the walls of the room.
